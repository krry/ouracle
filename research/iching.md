## Overview

The **I Ching** (易經, *Yìjīng*, "Book of Changes") is one of the oldest divinatory texts in the world — its origins traceable to divination practices using oracle bones (Shang dynasty, c. 1250–1046 BCE) and yarrow stalks. The text as we know it consists of:

- **64 hexagrams** — each made of six stacked lines (broken yin ─ ─ or unbroken yang ───)  
- **The Judgment** (*Tuan*) — a brief oracular statement for each hexagram, attributed to King Wen
- **The Image** (*Xiang*) — a further elaboration, attributed to the Duke of Zhou
- **The Line Statements** — individual text for each of the 6 lines (if they are "changing" lines)
- **The Ten Wings** (*Shi Yi*) — ten appendices attributed (contested) to Confucius, providing philosophical commentary

There are also **4,096 possible casting outcomes** when accounting for changing lines (64 × 64 with primary + relating hexagram).

## Most Authoritative Translations

### 1. Richard Wilhelm / Cary Baynes (1950)
**The I Ching or Book of Changes** — Bollingen Series XIX, Princeton University Press  
- Originally translated from Chinese to German by **Richard Wilhelm** (1923), then from German to English by **Cary Baynes** (1950)  
- Foreword by C.G. Jung — the famous synchronicity essay  
- The dominant Western translation for 70 years; still widely considered the most literary and spiritually rich  
- **Limitation:** Twice-translated; Wilhelm was a Christian missionary whose theological lens shapes the renderings  
- **Copyright status: 🔒 Protected until approximately 2047.** Both Wilhelm's German (1923) and Baynes' English (1950) are in copyright. **Cannot be freely reproduced.**  
- The GitHub project `zzkt/i-ching` notes this explicitly

### 2. James Legge (1882)
**The Sacred Books of the East, Vol. 16: The Yi King**  
- Legge was a 19th-century Scottish sinologist; his translation is the earliest serious English scholarly version  
- More literal and philologically careful than Wilhelm  
- Legge himself was skeptical of divination ("Of course all divination is vain")  
- **Copyright status: ✅ PUBLIC DOMAIN.** Published 1882. Fully available at sacred-texts.com  
- URL: https://sacred-texts.com/ich/index.htm  
- **Best freely available complete corpus for Ouracle.**

### 3. John Blofeld (1965)
**The Book of Change**  
- More accessible English; emphasized the Taoist reading  
- Blofeld was deeply immersed in Chinese Buddhism and Taoism  
- **Copyright status: 🔒 Protected.**  
- Regarded as excellent for practice but not the most scholarly

### 4. Alfred Huang (1998, 10th anniversary ed. 2010)
**The Complete I Ching: The Definitive Translation**  
- Translated by Taoist Master Alfred Huang; bilingual Chinese-English  
- Includes the Ten Wings in full (often omitted in other translations)  
- Provides full pinyin, etymology, and character-by-character analysis  
- Reviewers: "A careful comparison of Huang's translation with the Wilhelm, Legge, and Blofeld versions reveals its superiority in nearly every respect"  
- Includes "Ancient," "Modified," and "Neo-Confucian" casting methods  
- **Copyright status: 🔒 Protected.** Inner Traditions, 2010.  
- **Best complete scholarly-plus-practical modern translation.**

### 5. John Minford (2014)
**I Ching: The Essential Translation of the Ancient Chinese Oracle and Book of Wisdom** — Penguin Classics  
- The most modern major scholarly translation  
- Minford is an Australian sinologist who translated *The Story of the Stone*  
- Divided into two major interpretive streams: Bronze Age divination and Taoist contemplation  
- **Copyright status: 🔒 Protected.** Penguin Classics, 2014.

### 6. Richard Rutt (1996)
**Zhouyi: The Book of Changes**  
- Most purely philological English translation; strips away much later commentary  
- Focuses on reconstructing what the Zhou-dynasty text likely meant *originally* (pre-Confucian, pre-cosmological)  
- **Copyright status: 🔒 Protected.** Curzon Press.

## Commentary Traditions

### Confucian Tradition
- **Ten Wings** (attributed to Confucius, c. 300 BCE): Added moral, cosmological, and philosophical dimensions. The Great Treatise (*Dazhuan* or *Xici Zhuan*) is the most important — it frames the I Ching as a map of the universe
- Wang Bi (226–249 CE): Neo-Confucian commentary that stripped out numerological speculation and focused on ethical interpretation; hugely influential in China
- Zhu Xi (1130–1200 CE): Song dynasty Neo-Confucian; standardized the yarrow stalk casting method; his commentary *Zhouyi Benyi* is foundational

### Taoist Tradition  
- Views the I Ching primarily as a cosmological map of change (the Tao)  
- Blofeld and Huang both write from within this stream  
- The Taoist reading emphasizes *wu wei* (non-action), balance of yin/yang, and acceptance of change

### Jungian / Western Depth Psychology
- C.G. Jung's foreword to Wilhelm/Baynes (1950) introduced the concept of **synchronicity** as a non-causal explanatory principle for divination  
- Jung did 500+ I Ching experiments; his framework has been deeply influential in Western esoteric and psychological use  
- Richard Wilhelm was personally close to Jung; the two men deeply influenced each other
- This lineage (Jung → James Hollis → Robert Johnson) treats I Ching as a tool for accessing the unconscious

## Structural Notes for Ouracle

The I Ching's divinatory architecture:

```
8 trigrams × 8 trigrams = 64 hexagrams
Each hexagram × 6 possible changing lines = 384 individual line texts
Each casting = primary hexagram + (optional) relating hexagram
Total meaningful outcomes = up to 4,096
```

The **line texts** are arguably more oracular than the hexagram texts themselves — they give specific guidance based on which line is activated. Most online implementations and many translations focus only on hexagram-level readings, omitting line commentary. Legge, Huang, and Wilhelm all include line texts.

**For Ouracle:** Legge's public-domain translation provides the complete structural foundation. The Gene Keys system maps 1:1 to the 64 hexagrams, making the I Ching the connective tissue between Ouracle's systems.
