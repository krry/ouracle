## Tarot de Marseilles — Primary Tradition

### Historical Foundation

The **Tarot de Marseille (TdM)** is not a single deck but a family of decks sharing a visual vocabulary that emerged in 16th-century northern Italy and crystallized in 17th–18th century France. The canonical "Type II" Marseilles deck — what most practitioners mean by TdM — was largely fixed by **Nicolas Conver's 1760 Marseilles deck**, printed by his workshop and considered the gold standard for traditional reconstructions.

Key historical lineage:
- **Jean Noblet (Paris, c. 1659)** — earliest known preserved "Type II" deck
- **Jacques Viéville (Paris, c. 1643–1664)** — earlier variant
- **Nicolas Conver (Marseilles, 1760)** — the great standardizing deck; became the basis for all subsequent mainstream TdM decks
- **Paul Marteau / Grimaud edition (1930)** — 20th century popularization; Marteau's book *Le Tarot de Marseille* (1949) is the first modern French esoteric study; colors differ from traditional precedent (this is what Jodorowsky critiques)

## Most Authoritative Texts

### Jodorowsky / Camoin (Primary Modern Authority)

**Alejandro Jodorowsky** (filmmaker, *El Topo*, *Holy Mountain*) began studying TdM in the 1960s with Marcel Grains, a student of the Conver lineage. In 1993 he partnered with **Philippe Camoin** (direct descendant of the Conver printing family) to undertake a multi-year archival reconstruction.

Their collaboration (1993–1997) involved:
- Traveling Europe to compare historical decks and printing plates
- Computer overlaying of images across variants
- Culminating in the **Camoin-Jodorowsky Tarot de Marseille (1997)**, declared both a restoration and an original work

**Primary text:** *The Way of Tarot: The Spiritual Teacher in the Cards*  
By Alejandro Jodorowsky and Marianne Costa (2004, French; Inner Traditions, 2009 English)  
- ~500 pages; detailed symbolic analysis of all 78 cards  
- Jodorowsky's method: cards as a mirror for therapeutic dialogue, NOT prediction  
- Explores the "Metatron" — the systematic relationships between cards (triads, columns, rows of the major arcana arranged in three rows of 7 + The Fool)  
- Emphasis on reading the card as a living image, not a fixed meaning  
**Status: In print, copyrighted. Must purchase.**  
Publisher: Inner Traditions / Bear & Company

**Website:** en.camoin.com — Camoin has a website with some contextual material, deck purchase, and Jodorowsky's explanations. Freely viewable but limited.

### Paul Marteau

**Paul Marteau**, *Le Tarot de Marseille* (1949) — the first systematic French esoteric study of TdM. Foundation text, but uses non-traditional coloring (Grimaud version). Written in French; English translations are informal.  
**Status: Out of print; copyrighted. Some PDF scans circulate informally.**

### Etteilla Lineage

**Jean-Baptiste Alliette** ("Etteilla," 1738–1791) — the first professional card reader, created his own deck *Grand Etteilla* (1783) as a "correction" to Tarot, adding explicit cartomantic meanings. The Etteilla lineage diverges from TdM into more explicit fortune-telling (reversed meanings, numerical correspondences).

**Available freely:** sacred-texts.com has *Fortune Telling by Cards* (P. R. S. Foli, 1904) with Etteilla methods.  
URL: https://sacred-texts.com/tarot/ftc/ftc21.htm

### Papus (Gérard Encausse)

*The Tarot of the Bohemians* (1889) — foundational French occult Tarot study; links TdM to Kabbalah, Hebrew alphabet, and the occult tradition running through Court de Gébelin (1781).  
**Status: Public domain. Available on sacred-texts.com.**

### Court de Gébelin

**Antoine Court de Gébelin**, *Le Monde Primitif* vol. 8 (1781) — the origin of the Western occult tarot tradition; speculated (incorrectly but influentially) that the cards were Egyptian. Triggered all subsequent esoteric interest in Tarot.  
**Status: Public domain (French).**

## Digitized / Freely Available Card Interpretations

### Public Domain

**A.E. Waite, *Pictorial Key to the Tarot* (1910)** — all 78 cards described. Public domain. Best easily citable English corpus.  
Source: sacred-texts.com, Project Gutenberg, Wikipedia

**Rider-Waite-Smith deck images** — public domain *in the US* (Pamela Colman Smith died 1951). Not currently public domain in the EU.  
GitHub: https://github.com/metabismuth/tarot-json — MIT license, includes RWS card images with meanings

### Open Corpora / Datasets

**dariusk/corpora — tarot_interpretations.json**  
https://github.com/dariusk/corpora/blob/master/data/divination/tarot_interpretations.json  
Based on Mark McElroy's *A Guide to Tarot Meanings*. All 78 cards with upright/reversed meanings. MIT license.  
**Status: Freely available, MIT license. Best machine-readable corpus.**

**metabismuth/tarot-json**  
https://github.com/metabismuth/tarot-json  
RWS card data in JSON + images. MIT license.  
**Status: Freely available, MIT license.**

**enlambdment/tarot_corpus**  
https://github.com/enlambdment/tarot_corpus  
Web-scraped text from tarot guide sites; includes corpus from sacred-texts.com.  
**Status: Freely available.**

**tarot-book/tarot-api**  
https://github.com/tarot-book/tarot-api  
Structured REST API with decks, spreads, interpretations, card metadata.  
**Status: Open source.**

**Recommendation for Ouracle:** The **Flornoy/Heritage Noblet restoration** is regarded by scholars as the most historically faithful. The Camoin-Jodorowsky is more widely known and interpreted but more syncretic.

## The Wild Unknown by Kim Krans

### What It Is
The Wild Unknown Tarot was hand-drawn by Kim Krans in a spare, minimalistic black-and-white style with selective color, replacing human figures with animals and nature imagery. Originally self-published in 2012 as a limited run; went viral; published by **HarperCollins in 2016** as a full box set with guidebook.

### Authenticity / Originality
- **Not Marseilles-tradition at all.** The Wild Unknown uses Rider-Waite-Smith structure (78 cards, Major + Minor Arcana, four suits) but replaces its symbolism with a nature-based vocabulary
- Wands = snakes, Cups = moths, Swords = feathers, Pentacles = trees
- Major Arcana reinterpreted through animal and elemental symbolism (Death = a bare tree, The Tower = lightning strike on a mountain)
- Krans invented a new symbolic vocabulary inspired by intuition and nature, not traditional esoteric lineage
- The deck has genuine artistic power; its originality is that it feels mythically resonant without being derivative

### Published Interpretations
**The Wild Unknown Tarot Deck and Guidebook (Official Keepsake Box Set)**  
Kim Krans; HarperCollins, 2016  
- 78-card deck + hand-lettered, fully illustrated guidebook  
- Guidebook covers all 78 cards with Krans's own meanings, spreads, and introductory material  
- A cult object; the imagery is *the* interpretation — Krans is not a traditional reader, she's an artist-oracle

### Accessibility / Licensing
- **Fully copyrighted.** Published by HarperCollins 2016. All rights reserved.
- The imagery is Krans's original art; the interpretations in the guidebook are proprietary
- No open license, no permissive use; licensing would require HarperCollins permission
- **Not suitable for text extraction or derivative use without licensing**

### Assessment for Ouracle
The Wild Unknown has emotional and aesthetic depth but lacks scholarly or traditional grounding. It would add beautiful imagery but thin interpretive tradition. Its meanings are entirely Krans's invention. If Ouracle wants the *feeling* of this deck, the visual language (animals, nature, minimal line) could inspire design direction — but the text corpus is off-limits.

## Openly Licensed / Public Domain Interpretation Corpora

| Resource | License | Coverage | Notes |
|----------|---------|----------|-------|
| dariusk/corpora tarot_interpretations.json | MIT | 78 cards (RWS) | Best for NLP/structured use |
| metabismuth/tarot-json | MIT | 78 cards + images | RWS public domain (US) |

| enlambdment/tarot_corpus | Scraped/open | Multiple guides | Less structured |
| tarot-book/tarot-api | Open source | Structured API | Metadata + meanings |

**Key gap:** There is NO freely available TdM-specific full 78-card corpus with scholarly interpretations. The Waite corpus covers RWS tradition. For TdM, the authoritative text (Jodorowsky/Costa) is copyrighted. A TdM corpus would need to be written from scratch using TdM imagery as primary source.

## Summary Notes for Ouracle

1. **TdM is an image-first tradition.** Jodorowsky's method is to "read" the image directly, not apply a fixed meaning. This is philosophically aligned with Ouracle's potential approach.
2. **Waite's Pictorial Key is your public domain foundation.** All 78 cards, freely available, structured. But it's RWS, not TdM.
3. **The GitHub corpora (dariusk, metabismuth) are the most immediately usable** for building an oracle text engine.
4. **The Wild Unknown is legally inaccessible** for text use but valuable as aesthetic inspiration.
5. **TdM authenticity requires primary image engagement** — the visual language of the Conver/Noblet tradition is what makes it different from RWS.
