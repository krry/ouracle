## Overview

The **Oracle at Delphi** (the Pythia) was the most famous and authoritative oracle of the ancient Greek world, operating at the sanctuary of **Apollo at Delphi** (on the slopes of Mt. Parnassus) from approximately the 8th century BCE through the 4th century CE — nearly a millennium of continuous operation.

The Pythia delivered Apollo's oracles in trance-like states; priests would transcribe and interpret her utterances. The oracle gave pronouncements on war, colonization, law, and personal affairs to supplicants from across the Greek world and beyond (Lydia, Persia, Rome).

However, for Ouracle's purposes, the most relevant material is not the oracle's responses (which were situational) but the **Delphic Maxims** — a set of moral aphorisms inscribed at the sanctuary, attributed to the **Seven Sages of Ancient Greece**.

---

## The Delphic Maxims — What They Are

The maxims (Greek: *Δελφικά παραγγέλματα*) are 147 short precepts attributed to the **Seven Sages of Greece** (Solon, Thales, Pittacus, Bias, Cleobulus, Myson/Periander, Chilon), compiled by a certain **Sosiades** (otherwise unknown) and preserved primarily by the anthologist **Joannes Stobaeus** (5th century CE) in his *Anthology* (3.1.173).

### What Is Well-Attested
- **Stobaeus's list of 147 maxims** is the primary surviving source for the complete collection
- Three maxims are directly attested as having been inscribed at Delphi by Pausanias (*Description of Greece* 10.24.1): **γνῶθι σεαυτόν** (Know thyself), **μηδὲν ἄγαν** (Nothing too much), and **ἐγγύα, πάρα δ'ἄτη** (Make a pledge, and ruin is near)
- A **stele from Miletupolis** (modern Turkey) was discovered bearing Delphic maxims; scholar Louis Robert reconstructed that it bore the full 147-maxim list, with the final five on the base

### What Is Uncertain
- How many of the 147 were actually inscribed at Delphi — Pausanias mentions only three explicitly
- The attribution to the Seven Sages is partly legendary; individual maxims resist firm attribution
- Sosiades himself is unknown outside this attribution — the list may be his compilation of existing oral tradition

## Notes on Specific Maxims

**#8 Know yourself** — The most famous; cited by Plato's Socrates, Xenophon, Plutarch, Pausanias. Directly attested as inscribed at Delphi. Philosophically, this is not a psychological injunction but a reminder of human limits — *know that you are mortal, not divine.*

**#38 Nothing too much** — Second most famous; directly attested at Delphi. The Greek *σωφροσύνη* (sophrosyne — moderation, self-restraint) in maxim form. Counterpoint to hubris.

**#19 / #69 Oath-related maxims** — Two maxims deal with pledges/oaths; their relationship to the famously attested "ἐγγύα, πάρα δ'ἄτη" (Make a pledge, and ruin follows) is debated. Pausanias names this as the third Delphic inscription.

**#87 "Accuse the present"** — This maxim is particularly puzzling and has multiple interpretations: some read it as "take stock of what is happening now," others as "criticize the current state of affairs."

**#123 Hold oracles in awe** — A self-referential maxim deeply relevant to Ouracle's project.

**#143–147** — These five maxims form a developmental arc through the stages of life, from childhood through death. They were reportedly inscribed on the *base* of the Miletupolis stele when the stonemason ran out of room on the shaft.

## Structural Notes for Ouracle

The 147 maxims offer something different from all other Ouracle layers:
- **Imperative voice** — direct commands, not archetypes or symbols
- **Secular-ethical** rather than cosmological or spiritual (though the gods are present)
- **Age-specific** (#143–147) — a unique lifecycle dimension
- **Short enough to function as daily prompts** or oracle responses without interpretation layer
- The maxims are about *conduct* and *right relationship* — complementary to I Ching's situational wisdom and Tarot's psychological depth
